The patrol is the Met Office for the evening, and the shift ends with a
broadcast.

- Cloud casting: match the photo cards to the weather they bring - cumulus
  cotton wool means fair, cumulonimbus towers mean storm, stratus blanket
  means drizzle, wispy cirrus means change is coming. All of them form the
  same way: warm damp air rises, cools, and the vapour condenses. Point
  per correct match, bonus for naming how it formed.
- Air mass wars: deal warm and cold air mass cards against summer and
  winter cards and match the outcome - warm air in summer brings hot and
  sticky, in winter mild and damp; cold air in summer fresh with showers,
  in winter frost and snow. The sea card is the wildcard: slow to heat and
  slow to cool, it keeps coasts milder in winter and cooler in summer
  while inland does the extremes.
- Chart decode on today's real synoptic chart: isobars are lines of equal
  pressure and squeezed-together isobars mean wind; L is low and unsettled,
  H is high and settled; semicircles mark a warm front with steady rain
  ahead of it, triangles a cold front with sharp showers then clearing
  skies. Then how the chart got made: millions of readings from stations,
  balloons, ships and satellites - the same measurements on the map - fed
  through a supercomputer and sanity-checked by a human forecaster.
- Broadcast: pairs take tomorrow's actual chart, two minutes prep, sixty
  seconds to camera at the wall. Three marks to hit - name a cloud, point
  at a front, call the wind from the isobar spacing. Patrol scores each
  broadcast out of ten; smoothest anchor wins the weekend slot.

If the kettle demo runs, it is leader-only - steam at arm's length from
every scout.
