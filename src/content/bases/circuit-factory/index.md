Run the table as a factory: three circuit cards move down the line, and
nothing ships until quality control signs it off.

- Pairs build the line in order: a buzzer alarm (battery, switch, buzzer), an
  LED night light dimmed by a light-dependent resistor, and the digital one -
  a door alarm that only sounds when the AND gate sees both inputs high: door
  pad pressed AND system armed.
- QC inspects every build with two questions. One: what is flowing where?
  Trace the loop out loud - the battery pushes at 3 to 6 volts, around 20
  milliamps lights an LED, and the resistor is what stops it cooking. Two:
  what would you sell this as? Burglar alarm, fridge-door nag, automatic
  porch light - a believable use, or it goes back down the line.
- Stop the line at build three for the big idea: the LDR fades the LED
  smoothly through every level - that is analogue. The gate knows only ON and
  OFF, nothing in between - that is digital, and it is how computers think.
  Every pair states the difference back to QC before the alarm is stamped.
- Factory bonus round, sixty seconds: shout electronics that make life better
  - hearing aids, pacemakers, smoke alarms, the phone in your pocket. Point
  each, double for anything that saves a life.

First pair to three stamped builds wins the contract. Real factories solder
these onto boards - hot iron, fume, eye protection - which is why tonight we
snap instead.
