One lab rule, written big on the wall: predict it, test it, change one
thing, test it again. Lab books out - points only for predictions written
before the fizz.

- Rockets: third of a canister of water, half a tablet, lid on, flip it onto
  the tray and stand back. Predict the seconds to pop, then change ONE
  variable - more water, whole tablet, warm water - predict again, fire
  again. The tablet fizzes out carbon dioxide and pressure builds until the
  lid is the weakest point.
- Cabbage rainbow: line of cups of purple cabbage juice. Predict each
  liquid's colour flip before it goes in - acids flush it pink, alkalis
  drag it blue-green. Change one thing: double the vinegar, or water one
  cup down, and call the shade before you pour.
- Skittle race: skittles in a ring, water poured to the middle, warm plate
  against cold plate. Predict the winner, then run it. Warm particles move
  faster, so the colour spreads faster - change the layout or the
  temperature and race again.
- Sign-off rule: no station counts until the pair tells the leader the
  science behind it in their own words.
- Real-world close: each pair picks one station and gives the patrol its
  real-world twin - airbags fill with fast gas like the rocket, gardeners
  and doctors test acidity like the cabbage, tea brews faster hot because
  of skittle physics.

Most correct predictions in the lab book wins. Safety glasses on for
rockets, arm's length back, and nobody leans over a loaded canister.
