Five emergencies that carry no wound and leave no blood - the clues are
behaviour. Casualty actors play a patient file; patrols race to match the
right treatment card and talk it through.

- Asthma attack: wheezing, can't finish a sentence. Sit them upright and
  slightly forward, their own inhaler (show the spacer), calm coaching. No
  better after ten puffs or getting worse - 999.
- Severe allergic reaction: swelling lips, hives, tight throat after food or
  a sting. Their adrenaline pen NOW - practise the grip on the trainer pen,
  orange to the thigh - then 999 even if they perk up.
- Seizure: clear the space, cushion the head, time it, nothing in the mouth,
  nothing held down. When it stops: recovery position. Over five minutes, a
  second seizure, or a first ever - 999.
- Diabetic emergency: shaky, sweaty, suddenly confused or aggressive.
  Conscious and able to swallow: sugar - juice, glucose tabs, jelly babies.
  Not improving in ten minutes or unconscious: 999.
- Meningitis: the one to never miss. Stiff neck, light hurts, fever, and the
  rash that won't fade - draw "spots" in washable marker and roll the tumbler
  over them to run the glass test. Any suspicion at all: 999, say
  "meningitis".

Score diagnoses, rotate actors, and end with the point of the base: these
casualties look fine from across the room. The detective work is getting
close and asking.
