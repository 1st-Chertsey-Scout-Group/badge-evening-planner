How does the box know where it is? Three scouts and some string.

- Human GPS: three "satellites" stand on taped marks, each holding a cord -
  that satellite's measure of your distance, timed off its signal. Only one
  floor spot pulls all three taut; the receiver scout finds it. Diagram up:
  thirty satellites, 20,000km overhead, four in view for a true fix.
- Break it: one satellite steps "behind a building", cord bent round a
  chair, and the fix wanders. A point per real spoiler named: tree cover,
  tall streets, tunnels, deep valleys, too few satellites in view.
- Who owns it? The US military - built it, runs it, gives it away free -
  while Europe, Russia and China fly their own. Blitz round: a point per
  user society can't spare: ambulances, ships, rescue teams, phone maps.
- One spot, two languages on the HQ card: OS grid reference, the UK-only
  grid of letters then metres, against lat/long, degrees that work anywhere.
  Find both on the unit, then name its modern kin: digital mapping on
  phones, waterproof printed maps as the backup that needs no battery.
- Laptop finale: zoom geocaching.com around HQ and count the boxes hidden
  in plain sight. Walk one listing - coords, size, hint, log: find by
  navigating close then searching like a human; place only with permission
  and by the rules. Register the troop account - scouts pick the name.
