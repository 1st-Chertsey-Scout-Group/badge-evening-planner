A crime lab in evidence bags: real components to identify, a codebook of
colour bands, and a mystery only a multimeter can crack.

- Lab induction first: a leader mimes the crimes - chip rubbed on a fleece,
  iron left face-up on the bench, nose over the solder smoke - and the patrol
  calls each foul. Rules on the wall: touch something earthed before
  handling chips, the iron lives in its stand, fumes away from faces, hands
  washed after solder.
- Evidence bags out: a point per correct triple - name the component, give
  its job in one line (resistor resists, capacitor stores charge, diode is a
  one-way street, LED is a diode that glows, IC is a whole circuit in a
  black box), and match it to its symbol card.
- Detective details for bonus points: the stripe on a diode marks the
  cathode end, the dot or notch on a chip marks pin 1. Get them wrong in a
  real build and nothing works.
- Crack the safes: the colour code chart is the codebook - read the bands on
  four resistors and the printed code on two capacitors. Then the warning
  shot: every component has a rating, and pushed past it it cooks, sometimes
  with smoke. The rating is the safe limit.
- Multimeter forensics: battery, resistor and LED in a loop. Measure the
  battery's volts, put the meter in line for the current, then measure the
  three suspect resistors' ohms out of circuit. Volts equal amps times ohms
  - do the sums and unmask which suspect was wired in.

Most points takes lead detective. The iron stays cold all night - it is an
induction prop, not a tool.
