Patrol reports for astronaut selection: training floor on one side, the
entire solar system unrolled down the middle of the hall.

- PT first. In orbit the body skives: nothing to push against, so muscles
  waste, bones thin by about one percent a month, fluid floats up to puff
  the face - and the spine stretches a few centimetres taller. Run the
  circuit: thirty seconds per station of band rows, band squats and band
  presses on the mats, bands playing the part of gravity. Price of moving
  stations: shout one thing zero-g does to a body. Real astronauts pay two
  hours of this every single day.
- Unroll the universe: Sun at sheet zero, Earth at sheet three, Neptune at
  sheet ninety. Walk it. Then the bombshell - light, the fastest thing
  there is at 300,000 km per second, takes eight minutes Sun to Earth and
  over four YEARS to the next star. A light year is the distance light
  covers in a year: the tape measure of space. At toilet-roll scale the
  next star sits around 800,000 sheets past the door.
- Mission design: expedition sheets out. Pick a destination star, design
  the ship and what powers it, solve the time problem - sleep pods,
  generations aboard, something faster - then name the crew roles and
  choose the ten things in the hold.
- The pitch: sixty seconds per crew to sell the mission. Patrol votes for
  the one expedition that would actually make it - and the toughest
  question from the floor scores a point too.
