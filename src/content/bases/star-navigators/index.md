No compass tonight. The sky has pointed scouts home for ten thousand years
- this patrol learns to read it.

- Sky lock-on: find the Plough, the saucepan. The two stars at the end of
  the pan point straight at Polaris, the Pole Star - and Polaris is true
  north, always. Each scout finds it, then walks a north bearing across
  the field to prove it. Cassiopeia's W is the backup pointer; on a cloudy
  night, set the planisphere to tonight's date and time and run the same
  drill on paper.
- Black hole on a blanket: stretch the lycra drum-tight between four
  scouts, heavy ball in the middle - that is a massive star's gravity
  bending space. Roll marbles past: they curve, orbit, spiral in. A black
  hole is what is left when a giant star dies and collapses to a point
  with gravity so strong even light cannot leave. We find them by what
  they do: stars whipping around nothing, gas glowing white-hot as it
  falls, and ripples in space itself.
- Tech tour: how do we know any of this? Match the tech cards to their
  discoveries - Hubble and James Webb seeing back towards the beginning,
  Mars rovers driving the geology, robotic arms that built the space
  station, detectors that heard two black holes collide. Point per
  correct pair.
- Wonder round: each pair hunts one fact that genuinely amazes them - from
  the sky, the telescope or the fact cards - and delivers it to the patrol
  in one breath. Patrol picks its best to perform at flag-down for the
  whole troop.

Sweep the field for guy lines and holes in daylight; after dark it is red
torches only, so night vision survives.
